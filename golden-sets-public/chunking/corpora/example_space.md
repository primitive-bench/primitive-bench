# The Solar System

The Solar System formed about 4.6 billion years ago from a giant interstellar cloud of gas and dust. Gravity pulled the material together, and most of it collected at the center to form the Sun. The leftover material flattened into a spinning disk, and from that disk the planets, moons, asteroids, and comets slowly took shape.

The Sun contains more than 99 percent of the total mass of the Solar System. It is a middle-aged star that fuses hydrogen into helium in its core, releasing the light and heat that make life on Earth possible. The energy produced in the core takes a very long time to reach the surface before it streams out into space.

The four inner planets — Mercury, Venus, Earth, and Mars — are small, rocky worlds. They have solid surfaces, relatively thin atmospheres, and few or no moons. Earth is the only planet known to support life, largely because liquid water is stable on its surface and its atmosphere shields it from harmful radiation.

The four outer planets — Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune — are giant worlds. Jupiter and Saturn are gas giants composed mostly of hydrogen and helium, while Uranus and Neptune are ice giants with large amounts of water, ammonia, and methane. Saturn is famous for its bright system of rings, which are made of countless particles of ice and rock.

Beyond Neptune lies the Kuiper Belt, a ring of icy bodies that includes the dwarf planet Pluto. Far past the Kuiper Belt is the Oort Cloud, a vast spherical shell of comets that marks the outer edge of the Sun's gravitational influence. Many comets that visit the inner Solar System are thought to originate there.
